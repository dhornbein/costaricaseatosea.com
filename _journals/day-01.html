Day 1

Tidewater Canal
to Dona Yolanda's

George's Journal

Our hike begins on the 30th of November at a tidewater canal, but without sight of the ocean. Skittles is disappointed not to be able to dip his feet in the Atlantic to start off, and Orlando promises to fix that the next time we trek across the county. Our destination for today is Yolanda’s home 20 kilometers to the west.

The first kilometer from the boat dock is through the woods. Orlando points to a green toucan that we only spot when it flies off. We hear the high pitched warble followed by a strange rasping sound of an oropéndola, the beautiful bird with the golden tail that captivates us as it moves about the trees.

We hike through banana plantations for a couple of hours. Orlando tells us that when he was in his twenties, he spent two years working on this plantation and earned enough money to buy his farm. Workers get paid by production and can do quite well if they are skilled, quick, and don’t drink away their salaries. Orlando’s job was to cut bunches of bananas from the plants and hand them, a bunch at a time, to runners who took them to the cable line linked to the factory where they are cleaned and packed for export. The downside of the system is that if a banana bunch reaches the factory damaged the workers don’t get paid for it. A supervisor can tell if the damage is caused by the cutter, the runner, or the guy who delivers them via the cable line.

We get to kilometer 10 where we hope to see bananas processed at the factory, but the company supervisor is expecting his boss to show up and probably doesn’t want to explain why visitors are touring the plant. We have to peek at the process from a distance, but not so far away that we can’t see the workers prepping the bananas to the never ending beat of rock and roll music.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-01/IMG_0333.JPG" caption="Man dressed as a banana" class="has-inside-caption is-hidden-desktop mt-2" %}

As we leave the plantation the clouds lift, signaling that the rainy season is over and the long-awaited “summer” in Costa Rica will begin. When we get to the railroad line that at one time connected the port of Limon to the capital, we stop at Katy’s Market for a snack and something to drink. Orlando asks her if she could prepare a lunch for us, which she agrees to do. This kindness is typical of what we consistently experience as we head for the Pacific Ocean. Katy’s home is attached to her store and she invites us to have lunch in her kitchen and share the meal she had prepared for her family.

The towns along the railroad are named for the distance the rail line is from Limon. Katy’s Market is at the town of Mille 24. At Mille 28 Skittles stops at a Super Mercado and buys a lovely, blue Captain Marvel umbrella to shade his body that had become acclimated to the cool, sunless climate of Washington State. Drew bought a notebook and the two of them learn that no one can pronounce Skittles or Drew. So Skittles, whose real name is Rich, becomes Ricardo, Drew becomes Andres and I change from George to Jorge. So, in a matter of minutes three Gringos become Ticos.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-01/IMG_0372.JPG" caption="Walking down the train tracks" class="has-inside-caption" %}

Around kilometer 14 we are in for a special treat. Skittles spots a sloth about ten feet to our left. Orlando says that sloths climb down from their tree top homes to poop about twice a week. They dig a poop hole and cover it so predators don’t know they are in the trees above. We caught this sloth slowly retreating to the safety of his home.

The railroad trestle over Rio Madre de Dios is at 16.5 kilometers. There is a metal walkway on the north side of the tracks that should be crossed with care. I get the good advice to stop walking when I survey the pretty river below so I can keep myself from tripping and falling in.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-01/IMG_0382.JPG" class="mt-6 has-img-cover" alt="photo looking down on a foot as it gingerly steps on a narrow metal walkway rusted through with holes looking at the river below" %}
{% include img.html src="/assets/day-01/IMG_0422.JPG" caption="sculpture in Yolanda’s house" %}

We get to Yolanda’s around 4 pm, shower and change into our evening wear (which is the same as our day wear) for dinner. Yolanda fixes us a delicious, typical meal; rice and beans, meat, squash, fried plantains, and salad. This meal is distinguished by Yolanda’s meat course.

Yolanda’s meat recipe

  1. Take the chicken thighs; wash them in water and vinegar to eliminate the raw chicken smell and cut slices in the chicken so the cooking sauce enters the meat.
  2. In a frying pan add a couple of packages of Magi Chicken Consomme, Salsa de China and water. When the mixture is hot add unrefined sugar.
  3. In another pan brown the chicken in olive oil.
  4. In the first pan add chopped tomatoes and chopped sweet peppers. When the mixture is cooked add the browned chicken with its olive oil. Cover and cook; turning the chicken many times until done.

Dia 1

Drew's Journal

Drew's Journal

We started walking today. Woke on the floor of a strange house, friends of Orlando who live along a railroad track. We were served dinner and played cards with the owner. Breakfast of rice and beans (a Costa Rican staple), fried cheese, and Coffee. In the wet grey morning, a cab picks us up and struggles to carry us four giant men and our bags across the rough roads. The driver’s son sits in his lap as the 6 of us go some 8 kilometers east towards the sea. The futility of driving 20 minutes and walking back the same road for hours, a peculiar hobby.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-01/IMG_0312.JPG" class="has-img-cover" alt="photo of cows standing in a lush green field under overcast skys" %}

Light rain and grey skies. Flowers, birds, cows, and a gang of howler monkeys. We walk through a banana plantation. The plants are tied together with cord to hold them up against the gravity of their green bananas wrapped in blue plastic (to protect them from pests). A man dressed in a yellow smock, like a ripe fruit, sprays poison from a tank on his back.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-01/IMG_0351.JPG" alt="photo of banannas hanging from their trees, each bunch wrapped in a blue plastic" %}

We take a detour and visit the factory to see an endless stream of bananas washed and packed into familiar boxes. Orlando tells us that the worst job is packing the truck, Skittles tells us that his father is the one who unloads the tucks back in the US.

I feel fortunate to see the other side. As I walk with Orlando I try to find a thread of distrust with the Dole and Chiquita corporations (for like, the Banana Massacre and stuff) but he only offers a measured response of “some find bad things, others don’t”. It isn’t as simple as big companies are bad and the workers oppressed is it?

By noon we reach train tracks, the same we slept near the night before. While asking for directions to a soda (a small restaurant) the owner of a small market invites us to stay for lunch. As we eat she teaches us that the secret to good pineapple juice is to leave the pulp in the beverage pitcher.

A pallet of wood and blue bike lean against the fencing. A tall white man, Drew, stand near the front door." caption="Katy's Market" %}
{% include img.html src="/assets/day-01/IMG_0362.JPG" class="has-inside-caption" alt="A street side near the train tracks. Fencing and bars dominate the space. A bentch, probably a bus stop, holds the foreground." caption="Along the road and train tracks" %}

Bellies full, we begin our walk west along the train tracks. The sun is hot and the road is busy. Everyone we pass says hello, hola, buenos, or waves. Three gringos with large packs burning in the sun for fun. A strange sight.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-01/IMG_1505.JPG" class="has-img-cover has-inside-caption" alt="Photo looking down a road flanked by a train track. On the tracks a blue train speeds towards the photographer." caption="" %}

We pass piles of trash along the road. Orlando laments the sight. “The people here don’t care for their environment”, he says. I try to relate and convict my people, Americans, for their littering ways. He rejects the notion, and says that people from the states care deeply for the environment. It takes me a few more kilometers to understand my resistance to the idea.

Trashing

We walk past more ad hoc trash heaps. More plastic bottles and burn piles, bags of diapers. Shit, trash, crap, junk, waste. Stuff that will take thousands of years to disappear. It’s ugly against the lush and lovely greens of this land. We all grumble as we pass. What a shame.

Yet I come from the land of opulent gluttony, we use nearly a quarter of the resources but constitute less than a 10th of the world’s people; a single serving, quick and easy, plastic wrapped culture. We leave the TV and lights on for our pets.

Sure, the Ticos (Costaricans) trash the median but my people are trashing the whole world. We buy the bananas from this land. I know former executives from Coke who provide the plastic bottles that end up in the river. I fly down here to hike with my brand new synthetic backpack. Pockets filled with plastic wrapped protein bars.

Oh, I will put those wrappers in the “proper” place so they end up in some hole somewhere else. A landfill out of sight, in someone else’s backyard. I’ll cast the invisible garbage of methane from my burgers and carbon monoxide from my planes. My burn piles are on an industrial scale.

Look how these people are ruining the environment, I lament atop my throne of trash and ash. These people use in a year the water and electricity I use in a month. My home, which is buying local, trying our best to be good, is still pissing away 2.5 gallons of drinking water with every flush.

It feels like shame, it feels like hypocrisy, but mainly it makes me feel trapped. Trapped in a way of being divorced from reality. In the simulacrum, the alternate reality overlaps on a real life I can see but not join.
