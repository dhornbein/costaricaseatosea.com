Day 1

Tidewater Canal
to Dona Yolanda's

Our hike begins on the 30th of November at a tidewater canal, but without sight of the ocean. Skittles is disappointed not to be able to dip his feet in the Atlantic to start off, and Orlando promises to fix that the next time we trek across the county. Our destination for today is Yolanda’s home 20 kilometers to the west.

The first kilometer from the boat dock is through the woods. Orlando points to a green toucan that we only spot when it flies off. We hear the high pitched warble followed by a strange rasping sound of an oropéndola, the beautiful bird with the golden tail that captivates us as it moves about the trees.

We started walking today. Woke on the floor of a strange house, friends of Orlando who live along a railroad track. We were served dinner and played cards with the owner. Breakfast of rice and beans (a Costa Rican staple), fried cheese, and Coffee. In the wet grey morning, a cab picks us up and struggles to carry us four giant men and our bags across the rough roads. The driver’s son sits in his lap as the 6 of us go some 8 kilometers east towards the sea. The futility of driving 20 minutes and walking back the same road for hours, a peculiar hobby.

Light rain and grey skies. Flowers, birds, cows, and a gang of howler monkeys. We walk through a banana plantation. The plants are tied together with cord to hold them up against the gravity of their green bananas wrapped in blue plastic (to protect them from pests). A man dressed in a yellow smock, like a ripe fruit, sprays poison from a tank on his back.

We take a detour and visit the factory to see an endless stream of bananas washed and packed into familiar boxes. Orlando tells us that the worst job is packing the truck, Skittles tells us that his father is the one who unloads the tucks back in the US.

We hike through banana plantations for a couple of hours. Orlando tells us that when he was in his twenties, he spent two years working on this plantation and earned enough money to buy his farm. Workers get paid by production and can do quite well if they are skilled, quick, and don’t drink away their salaries. Orlando’s job was to cut bunches of bananas from the plants and hand them, a bunch at a time, to runners who took them to the cable line linked to the factory where they are cleaned and packed for export. The downside of the system is that if a banana bunch reaches the factory damaged the workers don’t get paid for it. A supervisor can tell if the damage is caused by the cutter, the runner, or the guy who delivers them via the cable line.

We get to kilometer 10 where we hope to see bananas processed at the factory, but the company supervisor is expecting his boss to show up and probably doesn’t want to explain why visitors are touring the plant. We have to peek at the process from a distance, but not so far away that we can’t see the workers prepping the bananas to the never ending beat of rock and roll music.
