Day 2

Dona Yolanda’s
to Escuela Las Brisas

George's Journal

We have gallo pinto (beans and rice), plantains, and eggs for breakfast, pack, say goodbye to Yolanda, cross Rt. 38, the main road from Limon to San Jose, and head up our first mountain on a gravel road. At first we pass large estates, gated and foreboding. As we get farther from Rt 38 the homes become more modest, and more colorful the less presumptuous they are. Near the top, we get a final view of the Atlantic. Orlando points to what he says is a cruise ship in the Limon harbor. His vision is better than mine and I can only imagine that I can make it out. I take a picture and enlarge it. Sure enough there is a white cruise ship in the harbor. I confess to Orlando that I couldn’t see the ship. “You do see the girl in the yellow bikini, don’t you?” he asks. “Yeh, of course I can see her.”

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-02/IMG_0434.JPG" caption="Orlando hiking up the first hill" %}

Skittles and Drew go on ahead and are taking a rest break when we catch up. To the west, fading in the distance like a Japanese wood cut, is a spectacular vista of the multiple ranges of mountains we will be crossing to get to Quepos, the Pacific Ocean, and the end of our hike.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-02/IMG_0450.JPG" %}

About halfway up the next hill Orlando spots a coconut palm, pulls his machete from his backpack and hacks open four pipas so we can have fresh coconut milk during our rest break. Like a bunch of little kids, we chase some tiny poison dart frogs about trying to get them to hold still long enough to zoom in, focus and get some pictures.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-02/IMG_0428.JPG" alt="Drew drinking from a pipa" caption="Drew drinks from a pipa" %}

We pass by the farmer’s pasture where Gail and I had camped out under Orlando’s tarp the year before. We shared the pasture with a hungry horse that chomped away during the night and a huge pig that was being saved for the Christmas meal. Gail asked Orlando how they slaughter pigs in Costa Rica. “Either we shoot them in the head or hang them by their back legs and cut their throat so they bleed out,” Orlando said.

“The last way was how we did it when I lived in Brazil,” I said. “But one time I made eye contact with a pig who was bleeding out and when he looked back at me he said with his eyes, ‘why are you doing this to me?’ That was the last time I had anything to do with slaughtering a pig.”

Orlando said, “Something like that happened the time I was hunting monkeys. We shot a monkey who fell to the ground and started stuffing a leaf in her wound to stop the bleeding. I raised my gun to kill her and she raised her hands like she was surrendering. But that wasn’t the worst. We shot a second monkey and when I went up to get her I saw that her baby was on her back. The baby started slapping her mom to make her come to life. I took the baby to the tree where her father was, but before I could get back to the mother the baby returned and started slapping her mother’s face again. Nunca mas. That was the last time I went hunting monkeys.”

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-02/Day 2 2017-06.44.30.jpg" class="has-inside-caption" alt="A massive pig grazing in a field where, beyond, a tarp tent stands in the rain." caption="George and Gail's camp from the year before" %}

But this time we pass by the farmer’s field. Our destination this day is a school where Orlando had arranged for us to spend the night camping out on the school patio. The trail leads down, up, down, up and a final down before our destination.

We are greeted by Waquin, the school watchman and our host. He is waiting with freshly brewed coffee when we show up around 3:30. Drew fixes dinner for the five of us; a hot meal from a just-add-boiling-water-dehydrated-dinner-bag, and finally a hot shower and rest.

Meanwhile Waquin, who must have been starving for someone to talk to, chats up Orlando. I can hear them still talking as I fall asleep.

Dia 2

Drew's Journal

Drew's Journal

I dream of being on a busy street.

I wake on the floor of Yolanda's. We arrived last night and were fed a delicious dinner and allowed to sleep on the concrete floor of a covered extension of the house. Walls of fencing. The Roosters started at 3am calling out to each other, checking in. We are served a simple breakfast with meat and cheese.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-02/IMG_0440.JPG" alt="in the forground a backpack and waterbottle adorned with a heart sticker. Beyond is a winding red dirt road through lush green." %}

Today we leave the flat lands of the coast and begin to climb. Coming from Denver the air here is thick, I feel amazingly strong. Up and up a simple road that becomes more simple with altitude.

I read Charles Esenstine’s essay The Ecosexual Awakening last night. I found it profound and validating in a way that only confirmation bias can. As I hike I wonder if perhaps some biases are better confirmed. The universe and all things are alive and conscious. We are not separate from this great tapestry. From the article:

“Could the story of Lover Earth be that new story for civilization? Returned from our journey of separation, we rejoin the tribe – the tribe of all life on Earth, the tribe of the living planet – and seek to contribute to the wellbeing of all.”

Reading this reinforces a feeling that I am on the right path, fitting as I start this hike.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-02/IMG_0443.JPG" alt="Orlanda sits infront of a lone tree forgrounded against the mountains of Costa Rica which fade into the distance." %}

We reach the top of the first hill, or mountain if you don’t live in the shadow of the rockies.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-02/IMG_0452.JPG" alt="A macro shot of stones and small shoots of grass, you can just make our the red leaf cutter ants hauling bits of leaf." caption="Leaf Cutter Ants" %}

The volcano Turrialba looms to our north. The road we hike turns to a small rough path along pastures. As the sun gets low in the sky we come to a small settlement and follow Orlando onto a school ground. We meet the caretaker and reconstitute a freeze dried dinner. I watch the leaf cutter ants work as the sun sets. There is running water and a warm shower here.

We sleep on the tile patio.
