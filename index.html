Drew Hornbein
On November 30th, 2018 I set off on an adventure with my 80-year-old dad, our friend Skittles, and our guide Orlando on a hike across Costa Rica from the Caribbean coast to the Pacific. I kept a written journal and captured the random tangents of a wandering mind.

{% include img.html src="/assets/day-00/IMG_1616.JPG" caption="Orlando with his grandson" frame="true" %}

Orlando Zamares
Orlando is an expert guide. He has crossed Costa Rica over 30 times, guiding groups as diverse as Outward Bound kids to solo hikers. Orlando was the first guide to cross the country on the new Camino de Costa Rica. Along the way he introduced us to many of the friends he has made over the years, who offered us meals and places to stay. He filled our hike with animal lore, reading tapir paw prints, calling quetzal birds into view, and recounting legends that have become part of the fabric of the Costa Rican people.

The Hike

Conchita Espino and Marco Marin are spearheading the creation and development of the Camino de Costa Rica.

Conchita says, ‚ÄúAfter hiking El Camino de Santiago Compostela with friends, it was apparent that hikers along El Camino bring economic well-being to the small towns along its route. It was not a big leap to think that a similar hiking route could be possible in Costa Rica. In Costa Rica most tourist dollars go to coastal areas in the Pacific and to some selected hot spots, leaving the rest of the country, and in particular, rural areas without good sources of income.

‚ÄúOur love for hiking, our wish to give others opportunities like we had enjoyed, and the great model that El Camino de Santiago provided are the principle reasons for embarking on the challenge to create the Camino de Costa Rica. We found our professional skills in organization development, strategic planning, and project management that we had acquired in our professions served us well. We also found fertile ground in many Costa Rican‚Äôs who had had a similar idea when hiking to Compostela, and we soon learned that the Costa Rican people in the small villages along the Camino de Costa Rica welcomed the idea and became instant partners in this endeavor.‚Äù
